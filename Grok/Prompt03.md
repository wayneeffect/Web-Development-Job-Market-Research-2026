**Entry-level jobs in web development** are roles designed for beginners with limited professional experience (typically 0–2 years). They focus on building foundational skills under guidance while contributing to real projects.

### Common Entry-Level Job Titles
Here are the most frequent titles you'll see in 2026:

- **Junior Web Developer** / **Entry-Level Web Developer**
- **Junior Front-End Developer** (most common starting point)
- **Junior Full-Stack Developer**
- **Associate Software Engineer** (web-focused)
- **Front-End Developer I** or **Web Developer I**
- **Web Application Developer** (entry-level)
- **Junior React Developer** or similar framework-specific roles
- **Intern → Junior** transitions (many companies hire interns who convert)

Other adjacent entry points include roles like **QA Tester** (with some web focus), **Web Content/CMS Specialist**, or **WordPress Developer** (easier entry for beginners).

### Typical Responsibilities
- Building and styling web pages with **HTML, CSS, JavaScript**
- Implementing designs using frameworks like **React, Next.js, or Vue**
- Fixing bugs and maintaining existing websites
- Integrating APIs and working with databases (basic level)
- Collaborating with designers, senior developers, and cross-functional teams
- Testing, debugging, and ensuring responsiveness/accessibility
- Learning the company's tech stack, version control (Git), and deployment processes

### Required Skills and Qualifications
Most roles expect:
- Strong **HTML, CSS, JavaScript/TypeScript** fundamentals
- Experience with at least one modern framework (React is dominant)
- Responsive design and basic understanding of UX/UI
- Version control with Git
- Portfolio of 3–5 personal or deployed projects (this is often more important than a degree)

**Nice-to-haves**:
- Basic back-end knowledge (Node.js, Python, etc.)
- Experience with Tailwind CSS, APIs (REST/GraphQL), or databases
- Familiarity with agile methodologies

**Education**:
- Many roles do *not* strictly require a degree (up to 2/3 of entry-level positions). A bachelor's in CS or related field helps, but strong portfolios and skills from bootcamps/self-study are common paths.

### Salary Expectations (US, 2026)
- **Average base**: $65,000 – $94,000 per year (varies widely by source and location).
- Realistic junior range: **$60,000 – $90,000** (lower in smaller markets, higher in tech hubs or high-cost areas).
- Total compensation can include bonuses/equity, pushing it higher.
- Remote roles are available but competitive.

Salaries trend higher for roles involving in-demand stacks like React + Next.js + TypeScript.

### Job Market Reality in 2026
- There are hundreds to thousands of "entry-level" postings across platforms like Indeed, LinkedIn, and Glassdoor, but competition is higher than in previous years due to AI tools and more self-taught developers.
- **Strong portfolio + demonstrable projects** often matter more than formal experience.
- Many companies value candidates who can show impact (e.g., "Built a responsive e-commerce site that improved load time by 40%").
- Government/contractor roles (e.g., clearance-related) sometimes have more entry-level openings.

### Tips to Land Your First Role
- Build and deploy real projects (personal site, clone apps, full-stack apps).
- Contribute to open source or freelance on small gigs (Upwork, etc.).
- Network on LinkedIn and attend local meetups.
- Tailor your resume to highlight projects and quantifiable results.
- Prepare for technical interviews (LeetCode basics, live coding, system design light).

**Bottom line**: Entry-level web dev roles are accessible with dedication and a solid portfolio. Front-end focused positions are often the easiest entry point. The market rewards those who can ship working applications and learn quickly.
